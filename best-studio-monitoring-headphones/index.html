The picks in detail

1. Audio-Technica ATH-M50x: the do‑it‑all default

Best overall
4.8out of 5

The ATH‑M50x has become a modern classic for a reason: it delivers a balanced, slightly forward sound that translates well across genres. The collapsible design and detachable cable add real convenience, and the clamping force is firm enough for a reliable seal without being punishing. It is the safest recommendation for anyone who needs one pair for both mixing and general listening.

Pros

  • Clear, well‑defined bass that doesn’t bleed into the mids
  • Lightweight and folds flat for easy travel
  • Detachable cable with three included options (coiled, straight, short)
  • Widely available replacement ear pads and parts

Cons

  • Clamping pressure can feel tight on larger heads during extended sessions
  • Ear pads can get warm after an hour or two
  • Slightly scooped upper midrange may flatter some problem frequencies

Who it’s for

Buy it if you need a single, reliable pair for mixing, tracking, and everyday studio listening – and want something that travels well.

Who should skip it

Skip it if you have a very large head or find firm clamping uncomfortable, or if you prefer a truly flat, analytical sound for mastering work.

2. Beyerdynamic DT 770 Pro: the closed‑back workhorse

Runner-up
4.7out of 5

The DT 770 Pro is the go‑to for closed‑back isolation without sacrificing clarity. Its velour ear pads are among the most comfortable in the business, and the boosted bass response makes it a favourite for tracking loud instruments. The coiled cable is virtually indestructible, and the all‑metal construction means these will outlast most of your other gear.

Pros

  • Excellent passive noise isolation for tracking and loud environments
  • Velour ear pads are breathable and comfortable for all‑day wear
  • Robust, repairable build with easily replaceable parts
  • Detailed treble that reveals sibilance and high‑frequency issues

Cons

  • Built‑in cable is non‑detachable, limiting replacement options
  • Treble peak can sound harsh on poorly recorded sources or for sensitive ears
  • At 250 ohm, the standard version needs a decent headphone amp to reach full potential (80 ohm version is easier to drive)

Who it’s for

Buy it if you need a closed‑back headphone for tracking drums or vocals in a live room, or if comfort and durability are your top priorities.

Who should skip it

Skip it if you prefer a neutral, laid‑back sound or if you don’t have a headphone amp for the higher‑impedance version.

3. Sony MDR-7506: the no‑nonsense classic

Best value
4.6out of 5

The MDR‑7506 has been a studio staple for decades, and it remains a benchmark for honest, unfussy monitoring. Its sound is forward and articulate in the mids and highs, which makes spotting vocal and snare problems easy. The folding design and affordable price keep it relevant, though the thin ear pads and coiled cable show their age.

Pros

  • Very accurate midrange and treble for critical listening and editing
  • Compact, foldable frame that fits in a small bag

  • Widely used in broadcast and post‑production, so your mixes translate well

Cons

  • Bass roll‑off can leave low‑end monitoring feeling incomplete

Who it’s for

Buy it if you are on a tight budget but still want a trusted reference for vocal tuning, dialogue editing, or any work where treble clarity matters most.

Who should skip it

Skip it if you plan to mix bass‑heavy music or need a headphone you can wear for four‑hour tracking sessions without readjusting.

Best for specific needs

Best for mixing and mastering

For critical mixing and mastering work, you need a headphone that reveals frequency balance without flattery. The Audio‑Technica ATH‑M50x offers the most reliable translation across systems, with a sound that is detailed and honest enough to make EQ and compression decisions confidently. Its detachable cable and foldable design also make it a practical choice for engineers who move between studios. Our pick: Audio‑Technica ATH‑M50x.

Best for tracking and loud environments

When you are recording in a live room or next to a drum kit, isolation and comfort become non‑negotiable. The Beyerdynamic DT 770 Pro provides the best passive noise rejection in this lineup, letting you monitor without bleed. Its plush velour pads and sturdy construction make it the default for tracking sessions that run into the wee hours. Our pick: Beyerdynamic DT 770 Pro.

Best for a budget starting point

If you are just getting into home recording or need a second pair for reference, the AKG K240 offers a surprisingly spacious open‑back sound for very little money. It is not built for heavy‑duty use or bass‑heavy mixing, but it teaches you the fundamentals of a natural, uncoloured monitoring approach. Beginners and students will appreciate the low barrier to entry. Our pick: AKG K240.

Our verdict

No matter which path you choose, the best studio headphones are the ones you actually reach for every session. If you need one pair to rule them all, the Audio‑Technica ATH‑M50x remains the safest and most versatile recommendation. For those who prize isolation and comfort above all, the Beyerdynamic DT 770 Pro is a worthy alternative. And if budget is your primary concern, the Sony MDR‑7506 or AKG K240 each deliver excellent value in their own ways. Pick the tool that suits your workflow, and your ears will thank you.
